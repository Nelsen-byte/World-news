I'm afraid, Mr. President, the threat is the death penalty.

“I’m afraid, Mr. President, the threat is the death penalty.” This sentence does not merely express fear it exposes a deep moral tension within the justice system. It forces us to confront a question that has followed governments for centuries: does the state have the right to take a life in the name of law? The death penalty is often presented as the ultimate deterrent, a harsh but necessary response to the most serious crimes. Supporters argue that it delivers justice for victims and prevents future atrocities. Yet fear does not always produce justice. In many cases, it produces silence, trauma, and irreversible mistakes. When the punishment is death, there is no room for correction, no appeal to time, and no possibility of redemption. History has shown that no legal system is flawless. Courts rely on evidence, witnesses, and human judgment all of which can fail. Wrongful convictions are not theoretical; they are documented realities. When the state executes an innocent person, the error is permanent, and accountability becomes meaningless. In that moment, justice itself becomes a victim. Beyond legal risks, the death penalty raises a profound ethical concern. If the goal of punishment is to uphold human dignity and protect society, then killing in response to killing creates a moral contradiction. The state, meant to safeguard life, becomes an agent of death. This blurs the line between justice and revenge, between law and violence. There is also the question of fear. A society governed by fear of execution does not necessarily become safer it becomes quieter. Fear discourages confession, cooperation, and rehabilitation. It hardens criminals instead of reforming them, and it teaches citizens that power, not humanity, is the final authority. Many nations have proven that justice can exist without execution. Life imprisonment, restorative justice, and rehabilitation programs protect society while preserving the possibility of moral accountability. These alternatives acknowledge the gravity of crime without abandoning the principle that human life has value even when that value has been violated. So when we say, “I’m afraid, Mr. President, the threat is the death penalty,” we are not speaking only of fear for criminals. We are speaking of fear for the law, for morality, and for the kind of society we are choosing to build. A nation is not measured by how harshly it punishes, but by how wisely it balances justice, mercy, and truth.

Sahrul Hanged Himself in His Room, Known as a High-Achieving and Diligent Youth

Sahrul Arif Wiguna (18) was allegedly found dead by suicide by hanging himself in a bedroom at his home. The young man was known as a high-achieving student at his school. In addition, Sahrul was also known as a quiet person who was diligent in his religious practices. Sahrul’s biological mother, Yuniati (49), said that during his lifetime her son was known as an intelligent student. Throughout his education at SDN Kenanten, from grade 1 to grade 6, Sahrul always ranked first in his class. “Because of my child’s achievements, when he entered SMPN 1 Kota Mojokerto his education was fully funded,” Yuniati told detikcom when visited at the family home on Saturday night (6/9/2014). Not only in elementary school, during his time at SMPN 1 Kota, the young man who enjoyed painting also achieved excellent academic results. “He was always in the top three of his class,” Yuniati added. Sahrul’s achievements continued when he enrolled at SMAN 1 Puri Mojokerto. Yuniati explained that from grade X to grade XI, her youngest son consistently ranked among the top three in his class. In grade XI, the student who chose the social sciences (IPS) major even became the winner of an English debate competition in his class. “His English ability was very good; from grade X to XI he always ranked third overall among parallel classes in his cohort,” she said. Meanwhile, Sahrul’s older sister, Fiska Jamriani (25), said that during his lifetime Sahrul was known to be quiet and devout. “Before he isolated himself, he prayed regularly and was obedient to his parents,” said Fiska, who claimed to be close to Sahrul during his life. However, Sahrul’s behavior began to change in the first semester of grade XII. Fiska recounted that her younger brother no longer felt comfortable socializing with his classmates in XII IPS 4. Sahrul said he was being ostracized by his classmates. “Because of that, before the midterm exams (UTS), my brother asked to transfer schools, saying he could no longer stand studying there,” Fiska explained. The suicide that occurred on Saturday (6/9) was allegedly carried out because the victim felt frustrated. Despite moving from one school to another, Sahrul felt ostracized and disliked by his peers. Sahrul also left a farewell letter.
